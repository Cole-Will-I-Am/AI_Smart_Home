# studies/

Offline analyses that motivate or calibrate the engine's tiers. These are **not** part of the
stdlib-only core and are **not** run by the test suite; they use numpy/scipy/matplotlib and are
kept here as reproducible evidence.

## `phm_home.py` — prognostics calibration study

The offline counterpart to the **prognostics tier** (`homeops/prognostics.py`). The engine tier
runs a lightweight, deterministic, stdlib-only online estimator; this study establishes that the
underlying method is *honest* before it is trusted, using the full inverse-Gaussian first-passage
treatment the core deliberately omits.

Latent degradation is a load-driven Wiener process; hard failure is first passage to a threshold;
observation adds a load confound and sensor noise. A Kalman filter over `[level, rate]` yields an
RUL predictive distribution with a reject-option. Findings (fleet Monte-Carlo):

- **Calibration is the linchpin.** On a right-censored fleet, the 80% RUL interval covers ~0.89
  of true failures (near-uniform PIT), at a **0.0%** healthy-unit false-alarm rate; median warning
  ~11 days.
- **Robust to misspecification.** With truth generated by a load-dependent Ornstein-Uhlenbeck
  process and the (wrong) Wiener model fit anyway, coverage holds at ~0.86.
- **It declines when it should.** On a low-observability failure (refrigerant loss aliased by
  weather) the estimator refuses to call ~54% of units rather than emit a confident wrong number.
- **The hype has a measurable edge.** With a perfect load model, false alarms stay at zero as
  load–signal coupling rises; with an *imperfect* (60%) load model they climb to ~3.7% — trust
  degrades exactly with load-model error, which is why a load-confounded proxy sensor cannot match
  a dedicated one.

Run: `python3 studies/phm_home.py` (prints a short calibration summary).
